# Legal, Ethics, OPSEC, and Well-being Reference

This is not legal advice. The investigator's jurisdiction, the subject's jurisdiction, and the data's jurisdiction may all matter. Read this file before collection when jurisdictions cross borders, brokers/CAI are in scope, systematic monitoring of a third party is proposed, graphic content is possible, or managed-attribution questions arise.

## Ethics checklist

- Consent or public interest exists and is documented.
- The collection is necessary and proportionate to the approved purpose.
- The subject's privacy and safety risks are considered before collection and before reporting.
- Sensitive personal details are redacted unless directly necessary and justified.
- AI assistance is disclosed where relevant and never used as evidence.
- Uncertainty is explicit; unsupported allegations are not stated as facts.
- The report includes limitations and what was not found.

## US awareness

- CFAA: do not access systems without authorization or exceed authorized access.
- State privacy, stalking, harassment, consumer-privacy, and public-records laws vary widely.
- CCPA/CPRA and the Delete Act (DROP) matter for data-broker remediation and California subjects (see `remediation.md`).
- Fair use, defamation, and privacy torts can matter when republishing or reporting.
- Commercially Available Information (CAI) — brokered identity/location data — carries heightened privacy and civil-liberties risk (ODNI 2023 CAI report context). Treat broker data as high-risk personal data and as leads only.

## UK awareness

- ECHR Article 8 / Human Rights Act: private and family life matters even when some information is public.
- Apply the JAPAN test: Justified, Authorised, Proportionate, Auditable, Necessary.
- RIPA / Investigatory Powers Act risk: passive public viewing differs from systematic monitoring, retained pattern-of-life collection, or interaction under a false identity. The threshold for regulated surveillance can be low — repeated, scheduled monitoring of a third party may cross it.
- UK GDPR / Data Protection Act: lawful basis, minimization, purpose limitation, and data-subject rights can apply to personal-data processing.

## EU awareness

- GDPR can apply extraterritorially to personal data of people in the EEA. Publicly available data is not exempt from data-protection duties.
- EU Charter Article 7 and ECHR Article 8 protect private life.
- The EU AI Act and data-protection rules constrain certain automated scraping, biometric categorization, and face-recognition database practices (untargeted scraping of facial images is prohibited).

## Managed attribution and OPSEC

Managed attribution means controlling what your research activity reveals about you and avoiding contaminating the subject (e.g., appearing in "viewed your profile" lists, triggering suggestions).

Allowed:
- Dedicated browser profiles, containers, VMs, or cloud research browsers for passive research.
- Separate personal and research accounts/devices where lawful and ethical.
- A VPN or network separation for investigator safety.
- Avoiding login to personal accounts while researching sensitive subjects.
- Archiving public evidence before it changes or disappears.

Not allowed:
- Deceptive elicitation, befriending, relationship-building, or any interaction designed to extract information.
- Impersonating a real person, institution, journalist, lawyer, official, employer, or associate.
- Creating personas to bypass privacy boundaries or trigger private disclosures.

The line: a **non-interactive research persona** (a neutral account used only to view public content where terms allow) is standard tradecraft; a **deceptive persona that interacts with the subject or their network** is prohibited here and is regulated law-enforcement territory.

## Psychosocial safety

OSINT can expose the researcher to graphic, violent, abusive, or distressing content.

- Warn before viewing or summarizing graphic material.
- Use low-exposure viewing: mute audio, disable autoplay, thumbnails, blur, grayscale, smaller windows, time limits.
- Take breaks; avoid repeated exposure loops; separate research time/devices from personal time/devices.
- Seek peer or professional support if material causes intrusive thoughts, anxiety, sleep disruption, or emotional numbing (Bellingcat/Dart Centre guidance).

A note for self-audits: discovering your own exposure (addresses listed, breaches, old content) can be distressing. Frame findings with remediation steps, not alarm; most exposure is reducible (see `remediation.md`).
